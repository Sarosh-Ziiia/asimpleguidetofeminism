What does feminism mean for us

Today, feminism exists as a means to end gender-specific oppression in all aspects of life. It serves as a voice for all abused women who have been made victims of patriarchy, be it social, domestic, or political. It exists as a movement to celebrate solidarity among women who have endured unimaginable trauma on the mere basis of their sex. Women who have had their autonomy stolen from them by men who justify it with a delusion of entitlement based on their sex.
With a global count of 15 million adolescent girls falling victim to sexual violence and 16 million girls married before their eighteenth birthday, it becomes a matter of pity that, after decades of innovation, global organizations and political parties have failed to act on this matter with the urgency it requires and deserves. Feminism becomes exactly what this world requires when we absorb the extent of gender inequality that prevails unaccounted for in our daily lives.
Without feminism, women today would be living without the fundamental right to vote. With the severe magnitude of victim blaming and rape culture, extensive underrepresentation of women across all political forums, and a significant wage gap existing on the mere basis of sex, feminism becomes nothing short of a necessity in the modern world.


Brief history

In the pages of history, the journey of feminism unfolds as a resounding anthem of resilience and liberation. From the suffragettes who fought for the right to vote, to the trailblazing feminists who shattered glass ceilings and blazed trails in every realm of human endeavor, the narrative of feminism echoes with the power of change.
It emerged from the depths of oppression and discrimination, a response to the pervasive belief that women were lesser, voiceless, and confined to narrow roles. Brave women and enlightened allies challenged the status quo, embarking on a courageous quest to dismantle the shackles that bound them.
With unwavering determination, feminists united, demanding equal rights, autonomy, and agency over their bodies and lives. They faced backlash, ridicule, and resistance, but their spirit could not be extinguished. Rallying around the core principle of equality, they forged alliances, created powerful movements, and paved the way for generations to come.
Feminism ignited a revolution in the hearts and minds of people worldwide. It sparked conversations, shattered stereotypes, and unearthed the deep-rooted biases that hindered progress. Through impassioned speeches, compelling literature, and unyielding activism, feminists propelled society forward, carving a path towards a more just and inclusive world.
In the relentless pursuit of gender equity, feminists fought for reproductive rights, economic empowerment, and an end to gender-based violence. They championed intersectionality, recognizing that the experiences of women intersect with race, class, sexuality, and more, making their struggle a tapestry woven with myriad threads of inequality.
Today, feminism continues to evolve, embracing diverse voices and confronting new challenges.It embraces the power of solidarity, as people of all genders unite to dismantle patriarchal systems and create a future free from discrimination and oppression.
The empowering history of feminism teaches us that change is possible, that our collective voice can break barriers, and that equality is not a distant dream but a tangible reality within our grasp. Let us honor the legacy of those who came before us, and with every step forward, carve a brighter path for future generations, where the power and potential of every individual, regardless of gender, can shine unencumbered.

    The urgency of the matter

  • 1 in 3 women worldwide have experienced physical and/or sexual violence in their lifetime.
  • Globally, as many as 38% of murders of women are committed by a male partner.
  • Each year, 15 million girls are married before the age of 18. That is 28 girls every minute.
  • As many as 1 in 4 women experience physical and/or sexual violence during pregnancy
  • Over 700 million women alive today were married as children (under the age of 18).
  • Domestic violence is a global problem that affects 35% of women worldwide.

    Women of tomorrow

  1. Malala Yousafzai

    Malala Yousafzai is a name that resonates with courage, resilience, and unwavering determination. Born on July 12, 1997, in Mingora, Pakistan, Malala defied societal norms and advocated for girls' education in the face of adversity. Her journey from a young activist to a globally recognized symbol of education rights is nothing short of remarkable. At the age of 15, she survived a near-fatal attack by the Taliban for speaking out against their ban on girls' education. Undeterred by the violence inflicted upon her, Malala continued her fight and became the youngest-ever Nobel Prize laureate at the age of 17. Through her compelling speeches, books, and activism, Malala has inspired millions worldwide and continues to be a powerful voice for the education of all children, especially girls, ensuring that their voices are heard and their dreams are fulfilled.
    "How dare the Taliban take away my basic right to education?""
  2. Jane Fonda

    Jane Fonda is an iconic figure who has left an indelible mark on the worlds of film, fitness, and activism. Born on December 21, 1937, in New York City, Jane emerged as a prominent actress during the 1960s and 1970s, captivating audiences with her talent and beauty. She has won numerous accolades, including two Academy Awards, and is known for her versatile performances in films such as "Klute," "Coming Home," and "On Golden Pond." Beyond her cinematic achievements, Jane Fonda is also recognized for her outspoken activism and advocacy work. From her involvement in the civil rights movement to her unwavering opposition to the Vietnam War, she has fearlessly used her platform to champion social and political causes. Later in her career, Jane became renowned for her influential fitness videos, which revolutionized the exercise industry and emphasized the importance of physical well-being. With her multifaceted career and unwavering dedication to causes she believes in, Jane Fonda has become an enduring symbol of talent, activism, and empowerment.
    “For so long, it seemed like if you said you were a feminist, you were anti-men. I think that’s dissipated for the most part now. I hope that men understand that the fight for equality benefits everyone, and that it’s in everyone’s best interest to have men and women be able to shoulder the same societal responsibilities at every single level - economically, militarily, spiritually, parentally.”
  3. Gloria Steinem

    Gloria Steinem is a feminist icon and a pivotal figure in the fight for gender equality and women's rights. Born on March 25, 1934, in Toledo, Ohio, Steinem has dedicated her life to challenging societal norms and advocating for gender justice. With her sharp intellect, eloquence, and unwavering commitment, she has become one of the most influential voices in feminist activism. Steinem co-founded Ms. magazine, a groundbreaking publication that became a powerful platform for women's voices and perspectives. Through her writing, public speaking, and organizing, she has tirelessly worked to dismantle the barriers that women face, including reproductive rights, workplace discrimination, and violence against women. Steinem's activism extends beyond the United States, as she has played a crucial role in global feminist movements and has been a fierce advocate for women's rights worldwide. Her contributions have inspired generations of women to challenge the status quo and work towards a more equitable and inclusive society. With her unyielding determination and visionary leadership, Gloria Steinem continues to shape the feminist movement and pave the way for a more just and equal world.
    "We’ll never solve the feminization of power until we solve the masculinity of wealth."
  4. Susan Faludi

    Susan Faludi is an acclaimed writer, journalist, and feminist whose work has profoundly impacted discussions on gender, identity, and society. Born on April 18, 1959, in Queens, New York, Faludi has fearlessly examined the complexities of gender roles and the struggles faced by women in various spheres of life. Through her groundbreaking book "Backlash: The Undeclared War Against American Women," published in 1991, Faludi shed light on the cultural forces that sought to undermine feminist progress and reinforce traditional gender norms. The book garnered widespread acclaim and became a seminal work in feminist literature. Faludi's incisive analysis and meticulous research challenged prevailing narratives and sparked important conversations about the state of women's rights and the resurgence of sexism in society. She has continued to write and publish influential works, including "Stiffed: The Betrayal of the American Man" and "In the Darkroom," a personal exploration of her father's gender transition. Faludi's commitment to investigative journalism and her deep understanding of gender dynamics have made her a respected authority in the field, and her work has contributed to shaping the feminist movement and inspiring critical dialogue about gender equality.
    "Feminism remains a pretty simple concept, despite repeated—and enormously effective—efforts to dress it up in greasepaint and turn its proponents into gargoyles"
  5. Leymah Gbowee​

    Leymah Gbowee is a Nobel Peace Prize laureate and a courageous advocate for peace, women's rights, and social justice. Born on February 1, 1972, in Monrovia, Liberia, Gbowee emerged as a prominent figure in the Liberian peace movement during the country's devastating civil war. Faced with the horrors of war and witnessing the immense suffering inflicted upon women and children, she mobilized grassroots efforts to bring an end to the violence. Gbowee's leadership in organizing the Women of Liberia Mass Action for Peace, which brought together women from diverse backgrounds and religious affiliations, played a pivotal role in ending the war and ousting former President Charles Taylor. For her remarkable contributions to peacebuilding, she was jointly awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 2011. Gbowee's tireless advocacy for women's rights and her commitment to reconciliation and healing have extended beyond Liberia. She continues to inspire and empower women globally, amplifying their voices and fighting against gender-based violence and oppression. With her unwavering determination and transformative leadership, Leymah Gbowee has become a symbol of hope, resilience, and the power of collective action in creating lasting change.
    “You can tell people of the need to struggle, but when the powerless start to see that they really can make a difference, nothing can quench the fire.”
  6. Waris Dirie​

    Waris Dirie is an extraordinary woman who has emerged as a prominent figure and advocate for women's rights, particularly in the context of female genital mutilation (FGM) and child marriage. Born in Somalia in 1965, Waris experienced the devastating effects of FGM firsthand, enduring the painful procedure herself at the age of five. Determined to break the cycle of this harmful practice, she courageously embarked on a journey that would lead her to become a global voice for change. Using her platform, Waris Dirie has fearlessly spoken out against FGM, raising awareness about the physical, psychological, and emotional consequences faced by millions of girls and women. She has become a powerful advocate, collaborating with organizations such as the United Nations and launching her own foundation, the Desert Flower Foundation, to combat FGM and promote gender equality. Waris's unwavering determination and remarkable resilience have made her an inspiration to countless individuals, shedding light on the urgent need for global action to protect the rights and well-being of girls and women. Through her activism, she has not only brought attention to the brutal reality of FGM but also ignited conversations and driven change towards eradicating this harmful practice. Waris Dirie's incredible life story and relentless pursuit of justice and equality have solidified her as a true icon and an indomitable force in the fight for women's rights worldwide. Her impact serves as a testament to the strength of the human spirit and the transformative power of speaking up against injustice.
    "Women’s loyalty has to be earned with trust and affection, rather than barbaric rituals. The time has come to leave the old ways of suffering behind"

Feminism comes in different shapes and sizes


Liberal Feminism:
Liberal feminism focuses on achieving gender equality through legal and political reform. It aims to eliminate gender-based discrimination and ensure equal opportunities for women in areas such as education, employment, and politics.
Radical Feminism:
Radical feminism seeks to challenge and dismantle patriarchal structures by addressing the root causes of gender inequality. It critiques societal norms and institutions that perpetuate gender oppression and advocates for fundamental social change.
Marxist Feminism:
Marxist feminism analyzes the intersections of gender, class, and capitalism. It views women's oppression as a result of both gender and economic inequality, emphasizing the importance of economic restructuring and social revolution to achieve gender liberation.
Intersectional Feminism:
Intersectional feminism recognizes that women's experiences are shaped by intersecting forms of oppression, including race, class, sexuality, and ability. It emphasizes the interconnectedness of various forms of discrimination and seeks to address the unique challenges faced by marginalized women.
Ecofeminism:
Ecofeminism links the exploitation of women and the environment, asserting that the oppression of both is rooted in a similar mindset that devalues and exploits nature. It highlights the connections between feminism and environmentalism, advocating for a holistic approach to social justice.
Postcolonial Feminism:
Postcolonial feminism examines the experiences of women in the context of colonialism, imperialism, and globalization. It explores how gender, race, and culture intersect and how systems of power perpetuate inequality on a global scale.

These are just a few examples of the diverse range of feminist perspectives. It's important to note that feminism is
not a monolithic movement, and individuals may identify with and engage with multiple types of feminism simultaneously.
